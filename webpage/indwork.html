üåç Understanding Space Weather

Space weather refers to the environmental conditions in space as influenced by the Sun and its activity. When the Sun releases massive bursts of energy through solar flares or coronal mass ejections (CMEs), it sends charged particles hurtling toward Earth at millions of miles per hour.

When these particles interact with Earth's magnetic field, they create geomagnetic storms that can induce electrical currents in long conductors like power lines, pipelines, and railway tracks. This phenomenon, called geomagnetically induced currents (GICs), can cause widespread disruption to industrial operations.

Industrial workers across multiple sectors must remain vigilant during periods of high solar activity. Power grid operators monitor space weather forecasts closely, aviation authorities may reroute flights, and communication companies prepare backup systems. The economic impact of a severe space weather event could reach trillions of dollars globally, affecting millions of workers.

Did you know? The largest recorded geomagnetic storm, the Carrington Event of 1859, caused telegraph systems worldwide to fail and even shocked operators. If a similar event occurred today, it could disable power grids for weeks or months, affecting virtually every industrial worker.
